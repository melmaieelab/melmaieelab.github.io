Dr. Latha Melmaiee’s lab focuses on small fruit crop improvement for biotic and abiotic stress tolerance using physiology, genomics, and molecular breeding approaches. The strawberry project requires large-scale germplasm screening against grey mold and anthracnose fungi to identify the resistant genotypes for fruit rot decay (USDA-NIFA). This project also aims to identify candidate genes and genome-wide associations for gray mold fruit rot resistance in strawberries. This project heavily relies on a controlled environment for successful inoculations and screening germplasm. Some graduate students’ research projects focus on identifying the less toxic and cost-effective alternatives to chemical fungicides to mitigate grey mold and anthracnose infections. Another critical project from Dr. Melmaiee’s lab includes the study of heat stress tolerance in northern blueberries. Blueberries (Vaccinium spp.) are highly sensitive to heat stress; reduced fruit quality and quantity were reported when temperatures reached above 35°C. There are trying to understand the high-temperature tolerance mechanisms in blueberries by studying at the physiological and molecular level using untapped wild species, cultivars, and segregating populations.
